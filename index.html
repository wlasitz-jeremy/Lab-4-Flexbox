Being a Diver 1

Depth and Pressure

Underwater, you're under more pressure because water also has weight, which combines with the atmosphere's weight(pressure). Since water has more density and is heavier than air 10 meters/33 feet exerts the same pressure as the whole atmosphere. Therefore, the pressure increases by one bar/ata for each 10 meters /33 feet you decend(go down). Likewise, the pressure decreases by one bar/ata for every 10 meters/33 feet you ascend(go up).

Pressure, Air Volume and Density

Water can not be compressed so its volume and density do not change with pressure changes. Since your body tissues are mostly made of water, you do not feel the pressure changes on most of your body while diving. Pressure changes do change the volume and density of air(or any other gas). As the pressure increases - as you go deeper - a gas volume decreases becuase the gas molecules get compressed. The gas density increases because all the molecules are there, but they're packed into a smaller area. This affects all the air spaces in, or in contact with, your body. These include your ears, sinuses, lungs, mask, and when using one, a dry suit. You'll also learn that this principal affects controlling your buoyancy, how long your air supply lasts, and some important saftey rules. Air volume and density change proportionately with pressure. This means if you go from the surface to 10 meters/33 feet, you double the pressure to 2 bar/ata(1 air plus 1 water), a given air volume halves, and its density doubles. If you go to 20 meters/66 feet, the pressure is 3 bar/ata(1 air plus 2 water). An air volume would be one-third the surface volume, and density would be triple.

Being a Diver 2

Seeing and Hearing as a Diver

Lets look into how being underwater affects how you see and hear.

Aparent size and Distance

Water is about 800 times denser than air, which causes many of the differences between the terrestrrial and aquatic worlds. One effect of this is that it causes light to change direction slightly (refract) when it transfers from water into the air in your mask. This direction change magnifies objects by about a third. Dependign on your perspective objects look larger, closer, or both than they really are. you may reach for something and miss because it's farther away than it appears. With experience, you learn to compensate unconciously.

Color Loss and Scattering

Water also affects light by reflecting, scattering and absorbing it. This means that as you go deeper, it gets darker. During the day, at the most popular dive sites you usually have adaquate light. On most dives, it does not seem dark at all, though on others, like night dives or when the visability is poor, it obviously does. In these conditions, you can use a dive light - you'll get to do this during the Night Adventure Dive in the PADI Open Water Diver Course. Water also absorbs light directly, but not uniformly. As light travels through it, water absorbs some colors before others. The farther light travels through water, the more color it loses. As you go deeper, red is the first color absorbed, followed by orange, yellow, green, and finally blue. You see the same color lose when looking at distant objects. Distant objects tend to become more colorful as you get closer. Your eyes adjust somewhat for the color change, but shinning a light at depth brings back lost color and shows the difference.

Hearing Underwater

Water affects what you hear underwater, because it tramsmits sound more efficiently than does air. You will hear many new sounds from aquatic life, boats, divers, and other sources. Underwater sound travels over long distances. For example,a boat that you can not hear at the surface may sound relatively close underwater. Sound travels about four times faster in water than in air. This makes it difficult to determine its source - underwater, it usuallyt seems like sound is coming form all around, or directly overhead. You may be able to tell relative change in distance by a sound growing louder or fainter, even though you can not tell the direction.

Gas Narcosis

Gas narcosis is not thought to be harmful itself. The hazard is that it impairs the good judgement, clear thinking and timely responses you need to avoid and manage problems underwater.

Causes and Effects

Gas narcosis is thought to be caused by increased dissolved gasses in the body tissues slowing nerve impulses that travel in the brain and nervous system. It slows the communication between the brain and the body, and between different parts of the brain. Breathing air most drivers begin to notice narcosis at approximately 30 meters/100 feet. Narcosis differs, however, from individual to individual and in the same diver from one to the next. Being tired, dehydrated or generally fatigued may make narcosis noticable shallower, and some prescription and over-the-counter-drugs combine their effects with narcosis, making intoxication stronger. Cool water, darkness, and limited visability seem to increase narcosis, though it may be that added stress simply makes the effects more noticable. Diving experience seems to reduce narcosis - but physcologists think you learn to compensate for it.

Avoiding and Managing Gas Narcosis

You avoid gas narcosis by not diving too deep. For the most part people, assuming no predisposing factors, gas narcosis isn't likely to be a concern diving as deep as 30 meters/100 feet. If you experience narcosis, immediately ascend to a shallower depth. Generally narcosis fades quickly.

*Equipment*

Equipment 1

Primary Considerations

The three most important considerations when choosing scuba gear are sustainability, fit, and comfort.

Sunstainability means the equipment is appropriate for you and the dive. If planning a dive in cool water,your short sleeve wet suit - perfectly suitable for tropicaldiving - may not be suitable.

Fit means the equipment is sized and adjested for you. Many pieces of gear will not function, or function poorly, if they do not fit properly. Your PADI dive shop and instructor will help you learn to select gear that fits and adjust it.

Comfort means you can wear the item for an hour or more without a significant distraction due to its feel or configuration. Uncomfortable equipment that annoys you can take the fun out of a dive. It can sometimes distract you from safety issues. Be sure you gear fits and it's comfortable. To a point, a porfessional can tell if something fits, but you have to say wether it is or is not comfortable.

Secondary Considerations

Once your gear is suitable, comfortable and fits, there are four secondary considerations: cost and features, servicability, color and style, and accessories.

Cost and features - You can coose gear from a price range, with more comfortable features on high-end models.

Servicability - Consider that some equipment requires annual or biannual professional servicing. Most dive operations service the brands they sell, so getting gear from your PADI dive shop typically covers this.

Color and Style - You can choose gear that looks good and is color coordinated.

Accessories - For each piece of equipment, your local PADI Professional can sugest related accessories. A professional at your dive shop may, for example, suggest retainters that hold equipment where it belongs, or an appropriate mask defog so you can see clearly. These contribute significantly to diving comfort and convenience. Do not neglect useful accessories.
